Naawi Oodena

Naawi-Oodena, previously known as the Kapyong Barracks and meaning “centre of the heart of the community” in Anishinaabe, has become a proud landmark of Winnipeg and a trailblazer for urban Indigenous reserves. Located in southwest Winnipeg, just 7 km from downtown, the 160-acre site was redeveloped through a partnership between the Treaty One Nations and the Government of Canada, with 68% of the land allocated to the Treaty One Nations and 32% to Canada Lands.

After nearly two years of joint planning and public engagement, the master plan for the site was released on March 2021. The vision includes green space, recreation facilities, a variety of housing, and up to 1.2 million square feet of commercial and retail space—accommodating between 2,300 and 3,000 homes. since completion, Naawi-Oodena now stands as the largest multi-use development in modern Winnipeg history, and the single largest urban Indigenous economic zone in Canada.

Residential (lower density and medium density)


Medium Density - Located along Kenaston Boulevard.

Medium-density residential areas—known by having a balance of building scale and open space, having between 20 and 50 units per hectare (roughly 5,000 to 12,500 people per square kilometer)—are designed to promote well-planned, multi-unit housing that offers a variety of options for people of all ages, abilities, and income levels. These areas are meant to support more people with well-connected housing, good walkability, and easy access to things like transit and shops—making them a good fit for busier roads like Kenaston Boulevard. It achieves this by:

  • Diverse housing options: Providing a mix of housing types with varying unit sizes, a range of tenure and lease models, accessible and age-friendly designs, and affordable homes that accommodate all income levels.
  • Quality Design: Features high-quality architecture and modern design, ensuring new development contributes to a strong and cohesive community identity.
  • Pedestrian Permeability: The design reinforces the relationship between buildings and the street by maintaining a high calibre of passability and activation at ground level.
  • Visual Quality: Minimizes the visual impact of parking, service areas, and similar elements on the public realm.

This area includes;

  • Low and mid-rise apartments, usually up to 6 storeys or 20 metres in height (whichever is less),
  • Multi-unit buildings like stacked townhomes,
  • Live-work units, which combine living space and small-scale business use,
  • And small, at-grade convenience stores or services, especially when built into larger residential buildings.


Lower Density - Located along Kenaston Boulevard

Lower-density residential areas - have a more spacious built form and reduced population density, having less than 20 units per hectare (about 5,000 people per square kilometre) – promoting well-integrated houses that complement other nearby houses to create a safe neighbourhood feeling, as well as safety, design, and cleanliness. These areas are meant to keep things quieter and more relaxed, with more space between homes and fewer people. They fit best next to existing neighbourhoods where things are already calm, like the western edge of the site, helping new homes blend in without changing the feel of the area. This is done by:

  • Diverse Housing Options: Providing a variety of housing types with different unit sizes, tenure models, accessible and age-friendly options, and affordable housing to meet a range of household income levels.
  • Quality Design: Encouraging good architecture and neighbourhood-friendly design to support a strong and lasting community character.
  • Visual Quality: Limiting the impact of parking, service areas, and similar elements to keep the public areas looking clean and welcoming.

This area includes;

  • Single-detached and semi-detached houses,
  • Townhouses and stacked townhomes,
  • Small multi-unit buildings like triplexes or fourplexes (sometimes called plex-type housing),
  • And secondary suites, such as basement apartments or backyard units.

Most buildings in these areas are up to 3.5 storeys or 12 metres tall, whichever is less, helping preserve the neighbourhood scale. Densities generally fall between 10 and 18 units per acre, offering a balance between space and gentle infill.



Sports and Recreation

This area focuses on bringing people together for a variety of activities, from sports and culture to simply being active. It's designed to serve the entire community and even the broader region, with spaces for playing, gathering, celebrating, and staying healthy. Located along Kenaston Boulevard, it's easily accessible and well-suited for large events and activities. Here’s a breakdown of what’s included:

  • Community Recreation: The area offers large fields, arenas, and community centres for events like sports tournaments, outdoor concerts, and seasonal festivals. These spaces are open and welcoming, designed for everyone to enjoy.
  • Healthy Lifestyle: With trails, fields, and green spaces, the area provides plenty of opportunities for physical activity and relaxation. Whether you’re looking for structured exercise or just a leisurely walk, it supports all kinds of wellness activities.
  • Cultural Emphasis: The space includes versatile venues for cultural events such as performances and traditional gatherings, celebrating the community’s unique identity and heritage.
  • Landmarks: A prominent feature is the powwow arbour, a gathering space for cultural events that serves as both a meeting place and a visual anchor for the area.

Defining Features

  • Indoor and outdoor recreation facilities like sports fields, arenas, and community centres
  • Cultural and entertainment spaces integrated within the public areas
  • Nearby retail and services to support recreational and event functions
  • Designs that ensure year-round usability, with windbreaks, lighting, and weather-conscious elements
  • Clear pathways for pedestrians, cyclists, and vehicles, with easy access to parking, entrances, and future transit options
  • Parking areas located away from the front of buildings to maintain a clean and welcoming streetscape


Cultural Campus and Education

The Cultural Campus and Education area features be the heart of the community, where governance, culture, and education intersect. It’s built to welcome all people while celebrating Indigenous identity and heritage. Located along Kenaston Boulevard, this area is equipped to host meetings, classes, performances, and cultural events. Here’s a closer look:

  • Treaty One HQ: The main centre for governance and administration, featuring buildings that reflect the values of the Treaty One Nation and support its long-term goals.
  • A Place to Meet: The district is a gathering space rooted in First Nations values, designed for connection, storytelling, and shared experiences across communities.
  • Indigenous Innovation Campus: A mixed-use space that includes learning and collaboration, with facilities for educational programs, workshops, and creative spaces.
  • Cultural Excellence: The campus will host museums, galleries, and theatres to celebrate Indigenous arts and culture.
  • Reconciliation Through Education: The area includes programs and facilities that focus on truth, understanding, and reconciliation, placing Indigenous perspectives at the forefront of public education.

Defining Features

  • A diverse mix of institutional, educational, and cultural uses
  • Offices, hotels, conference centres, and residences to keep the area vibrant
  • Retail and service spaces that cater to students, visitors, and staff
  • Clear pedestrian, cyclist, and vehicular routes for easy movement throughout the campus
  • Discreetly placed parking areas to preserve the site’s design and focus on community engagement


Community Spaces

Community Spaces are designed to connect people through parks, plazas, and open spaces that promote both recreational activities and social gatherings. Located throughout the development and linked to Kenaston Boulevard, these spaces reflect Indigenous values, honour history, and support community life. Here’s what you can expect:

  • Connected Green Network: A system of green spaces, including parks, trails, plazas, and courtyards that links the entire site, creating a continuous network for people to explore and enjoy.
  • Year-Round Use: These spaces are designed to be used in all seasons, with features such as windbreaks, shaded seating, and lighting for evening use.
  • Built for Everyone: The design ensures that all areas are universally accessible, integrating art, cultural elements, and native plants that reflect the community’s identity.
  • Flexible Gathering: These spaces can accommodate a variety of events, from quiet moments to large community festivals and powwows.
  • Sustainable Design: The area incorporates stormwater management and uses native plants to create a low-impact, environmentally sustainable landscape.
  • Art & Culture: Public art by First Nations artists is a key feature throughout the open spaces, adding to the area's character and sense of identity.
  • History Remembered: Monuments and interpretive spaces will honour the military heritage of the site, ensuring that history is preserved and reflected in the public space.

Defining Features

  • A network of public parks, plazas, and gathering spaces throughout the site
  • Seasonal usability with windbreaks, shaded seating, and sun exposure areas
  • Universally accessible design across all public spaces
  • Low Impact Development features for stormwater retention and natural filtration
  • Pathways that link into larger pedestrian and cycling systems
  • Public art as a defining feature of the open space identity
  • Flexible spaces for evolving community needs
  • Commemorative elements that preserve the site’s historical context


Mixed-Use Village

Located along Grant Avenue, the Mixed-Use Village is functions as a vibrant, higher-density corridor that blends residential, retail, and cultural functions. This district features support walkability, includes future rapid transit infrastructure, and provide a welcoming urban entry point to the broader development. The goal is to create a dynamic urban environment that reflects community values while addressing housing needs and public space quality.

  • Mix of Uses: A wide range of land uses are supported, including multi-unit housing, retail and office spaces, restaurants, institutional facilities, and community or cultural hubs—ideally integrated within mixed-use buildings.
  • Active Streetscape: Ground floors are encouraged to host lively, public-facing uses, such as shops or cafés, with residential or office units above. This creates a pedestrian-friendly streetscape with constant activity throughout the day.
  • Quality Design: Strong architectural standards and thoughtful urban design will help ensure that new buildings contribute to a cohesive and distinct neighbourhood identity.
  • Pedestrian Permeability: All buildings must maintain active and transparent ground levels, with entrances, windows, and features that enhance the connection between the interior and the street.
  • Visual Quality: Parking and service areas must be hidden from street view—placed behind or within buildings—and front yards are to remain free of surface parking.
  • Diverse Housing Options: A range of unit sizes, housing types, and affordability levels will help support a mixed-income community. Accessibility and age-friendly design is included.

To guide implementation, specific policies regulate height, density, and access:

  • Height Limit: Buildings does rise to a maximum of six storeys, or 20 metres—whichever is less—with taller ground floors (4.5 m) to accommodate commercial use.
  • Density Target: Residential development within this district should achieve a density between 40–60 units per gross developable acre.
  • Access Control: No direct access from Grant Avenue is permitted unless through planned laneway connections. This preserves the pedestrian environment and supports transit access.
  • Transit Readiness: The site design must anticipate and accommodate a future rapid-transit station at Grant Avenue and Route 90.
  • Use Restrictions: Automobile-oriented uses such as sales lots, service stations, gas bars, car washes, and drive-throughs are prohibited to maintain the pedestrian and mixed-use character.


Commercial Mixed-Use

Situated along key corridors such as Kenaston Boulevard, the Commercial Mixed-Use designation is intended to support a robust range of retail, service, office, and cultural uses that serve both the surrounding neighbourhoods and the broader Treaty 1 and Winnipeg communities. This area will function as a vibrant, accessible commercial hub with high design standards and thoughtful integration of transportation options.

  • Diversity of Commercial Uses: Designed to accommodate both large-format retail stores and clusters of smaller shops and services, promoting a balanced and flexible commercial environment.
  • Clustered Cultural Facilities: Block B is identified for major cultural developments, supporting arts, heritage, and community activities that reinforce the area’s identity and cultural relevance.
  • Multi-Modal Access: Developments must prioritize pedestrian, cycling, transit, and vehicle access, with coordinated layouts that include drop-off zones, bike parking, and safe, well-marked pathways.
  • Quality Design: All buildings must be held to high architectural and urban design standards, with attention to massing, site orientation, and accessibility from public areas.
  • Visual Quality: Parking and service areas must be visually screened or located to the rear or sides of sites, minimizing their presence along public frontages and contributing to an appealing streetscape.
  • Neighbourhood Compatibility: Commercial developments are to be carefully situated to avoid encroachment or adverse impacts on neighbouring residential areas, with appropriate setbacks, transitions, and buffering.

The following policy directions apply to guide future development and implementation:

  • Permitted Uses: This includes a full range of retail and commercial services, such as offices, restaurants, personal services, food stores, pharmacies, hotels, cultural and recreational facilities, gas bars, EV charging stations, and upper-level or rear-facing residential units.
  • Height Limit: Maximum building height is capped at six storeys or 22.5 metres, whichever is lower. Commercial buildings assume 4.5 metres for the ground floor and 3.6 metres for upper floors.
  • Frontage Restrictions: At-grade residential units are not permitted along Route 90 to preserve a fully commercial street frontage and transit-supportive character.
  • Accessibility and Connectivity: Developments must offer safe and convenient pedestrian, cycling, and transit access, and ensure smooth internal site circulation between parking, loading, entrances, and public streets.
  • Commercial Typologies: A healthy mix of small- and medium-sized commercial units is encouraged, with limited inclusion of large-format retail to ensure variety and pedestrian engagement.
  • Coordinated Site Planning: Adjacent commercial developments are encouraged to be designed cohesively, sharing key features such as entrances, pathways, lighting, landscaping, signage, and access routes to promote efficiency and a unified public realm experience.


Community Design Highlights

Underpinning all public-realm and landscape work are six guiding principles, each supported by strategic directions to create a memorable, inclusive, and culturally rooted environment:

  • Iconic: Use native species, local materials, and Indigenous patterns; integrate a 1% public-art budget; reference military and Treaty No. 1 heritage.
  • Evocative: Design multi-sensory landscapes (sound, scent, texture); include natural play elements and ceremonial fire pits; frame memorable sightlines.
  • Nourishing: Incorporate community gardens, orchards, aquaponics, and wild rice in stormwater ponds; establish a social-enterprise nursery for interim plantings.
  • Indigenous: Engage Knowledge Keepers in design; integrate cardinal-direction symbolism; cultivate traditional tobacco and medicinal plants.
  • Education: Provide interpretive signage in planted areas; create hands-on learning spaces (gardens, shelters); support oral/aural teaching opportunities.
  • Versatile: Ensure winter interest with evergreen structure and snow-reuse for skating/tobogganing; include adaptable shelters, windbreaks, and flexible programming.


Sustainability & Innovation

A dedicated Sustainability Framework advances four core components, encouraging third-party certifications (e.g., LEED, One Planet Living) and local partnerships:

  • Sustainable Energy: Promote zero-net-energy buildings, district heating/cooling utilities, passive-solar design, geothermal and solar systems; source durable, locally made/recycled materials; engage community on energy conservation.
  • Sustainable Transportation: Target a 50% auto-driver, 15% auto-passenger, 21% transit, 14% walking/cycling mode split; design porous street networks, invest in AT infrastructure, bike- and car-share programs, EV charging; collaborate on Route 90 and rapid-transit integration.
  • Green + Blue Infrastructure: Integrate LID strategies (bioswales, rain gardens, green roofs); design stormwater ponds as public amenities with native planting; limit peak flows; includes private ownership/maintenance of SWMFs.
  • Community Health + Well-Being: Prioritize active AT routes; embed recreation facilities in Community Spaces; support social-enterprise procurement; maintain ongoing engagement with T1N and neighbours.


Cultural & Architectural Identity

Cultural expression permeates both built form and public art:

  • Architecture & Urban Form: Mixed-use and cultural campus buildings reference Indigenous design motifs, use high-quality materials, and emphasize human scale at ground level, reinforcing Treaty 1 identity with Ojibwe-named streets and plazas.
  • Public Art (Section 6.3): Establish a Public Art Advisory, dedicate 1% of capital budgets, prioritize First Nations artists, and integrate works into parks, streetscapes, and landmarks like the Cultural Campus gateway arbour.
  • Naming Plan (Section 6.5): Streets and parks carry Treaty 1 language alongside English, approved by a Naming Council with Knowledge Keeper input; ceremonial spaces and art pieces bear culturally significant names.


Infrastructure & Connectivity

An integrated Infrastructure Framework ensures phased, coordinated delivery of roads, transit, water, sewer, and utilities:

  • Transportation + Mobility (Section 8.1): Conducted a Transportation Impact Study; set mode-split targets; prioritize AT and transit; define street hierarchy with complete sidewalks, buffered bike lanes, traffic calming (chicanes, curb bump-outs), and flexible loading/drop-off lanes; plan rapid-transit nodes at Grant Ave. & Route 90.
  • Site Servicing Framework (Section 8.2):
    • Water distribution: connect to existing mains via multiple tie-ins; promote phased installation alongside street construction.
    • Wastewater collection: sized for peak flows plus inflow/infiltration; drains to Doncaster Trunk; staged sewer connections.
    • Shallow utilities: coordinate with providers for power, telecom and future-proofed distribution corridors.
  • Stormwater Management: Five engineered ponds integrated into parks; LID features across all parcels; native upland and aquatic plantings; adaptable layouts subject to detailed design.

Together, these components establish a phased, resilient framework that aligns land use, design, sustainability, culture, and infrastructure—setting a new benchmark for Indigenous-led urban development.
